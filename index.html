What is this site?

This is a guide to Berkeley Student Cooperative (BSC) budgets from FY 2007-08 through FY 2026-27, built for board directors. It compiles data from multiple board-approved budget spreadsheets.

What is the BSC? The Berkeley Student Cooperative is a student-run nonprofit providing affordable housing and meals to UC Berkeley students since 1933. Members govern directly and contribute labor (workshifts) for below-market rates. It is financially self-sustaining: no university subsidy. The BSC operates 17 co-op houses plus Rochdale, Fenwick, and Northside apartments, serving ~1,200+ members.
A note on the data: Figures for FY 2007-08 through FY 2025-26 are drawn from the prior-year columns in each BSC budget spreadsheet, the best available source, but not verified against final audited statements. FY 2026-27 is a board-approved projection. Treat all figures as directionally accurate rather than audit-precise.

Budgeted Occupancy Assumptions

These percentages were set in April 2026 using historical patterns and directly determine the revenue line in the budget. If actual occupancy falls short, revenue falls short too, and the deficit widens further.

Each term has a structural reason for its occupancy level: Summer is always low, members leave for internships, travel, or go home; the BSC competes for a smaller pool of year-round students. Fall is the revenue engine, new students arrive, waitlists clear, and houses fill close to capacity. Spring dips slightly, some members who signed up in fall don't return for spring due to study abroad, internships, or housing changes. New members can fill spots but the net effect is slightly lower occupancy than fall.

Room & Board Rate vs. Room Revenue: the occupancy gap

Rate up 47% since 2016-17 · Room Revenue only +11%

The BSC is charging more per bed but filling fewer of them a core driver of the structural deficit.

R&B Rate per semester

Total Room Revenue

The BSC has raised the per-semester R&B rate 47% since 2016-17 ($3,384 → $4,986), yet room revenue has grown only 11%, because occupancy has not kept pace. At 2016-17 occupancy levels, today's rates would generate ~$7.85M in room revenue; the actual 2026-27 projection is $5.91M, leaving ~$1.94M on the table, more than the entire projected deficit of −$1.30M. Revenue comes from all three terms (summer 43%, fall 90%, spring 84.75%), and every 10 percentage points of fall occupancy is worth ~$625,000 that semester (125 beds × $4,986). Fall 2026 is tracking close to budget, but the deficit persists even at full budgeted occupancy.

Member Rate Trends 2016 to 2027

Board-approved rates shown as the total per academic year (fall + spring, i.e. two semesters) for every room and board type, reconstructed from the rate-calculator tabs of the BSC budget files. Each budget lists both last year's rates and the new rates, which chain together across years. Two dashed benchmark lines show what the headline rate would cost today if it had instead tracked US inflation (CPI-U) (grey/black) or Bay Area market rent (teal, BLS SF-metro rent index) since 2016-17. Use the buttons to switch building, and click any legend entry to show or hide a line.

The pattern: Room & Board climbed from $6,768/year (2016-17) to $9,972 (2026-27) — about +47% over the decade, versus roughly +39% for general US inflation over the same period. Co-op rates have modestly outpaced CPI. Lines are flat where the Board approved a 0% change (2018-19→19-20, 2020-21→21-22, 2022-23→23-24); the steepest single jumps were 2024-25 (+9%) and 2026-27 (+7.5%).
Why benchmark against inflation? A figure like “+47%” means little on its own — almost everything costs more than it did in 2016. CPI converts the question from “did rates go up?” to “did rates go up faster than the cost of living?” Because members' wages, financial aid, and other expenses tend to move with inflation, a rate that grows only with CPI keeps housing's share of a member's budget roughly constant; a rate that outpaces CPI means housing is eating a growing share of what members can afford — the real affordability signal for the Board to watch. CPI is also an independent yardstick published by the federal government (BLS), not set by the BSC, so it keeps the comparison honest — and consistent with how this guide already benchmarks expense lines like insurance, legal, food, and water against their own indices. One caveat: general CPI tracks overall cost of living, not the Bay Area rental market specifically, so it answers “are rates outpacing inflation?” rather than “is the co-op still a bargain versus market rent?”
A second benchmark — Bay Area market rent. Because affordability-versus-the-market is part of the BSC's mission, the chart and table also compare rates to the BLS rent index for the San Francisco-Oakland metro. This answers a different question than CPI: not “are rates outpacing the cost of living?” but “are we still pulling away from the rental market we compete with?” Over 2016-17 → 2026-27, Room & Board rose +47%, while the Bay Area rent index rose +33% and general CPI +39% — so co-op rates grew faster than both benchmarks, even though they remain far below market in absolute dollars (a co-op room runs roughly half to two-thirds of comparable Berkeley market cost, before counting bundled food and utilities). The takeaway: the co-op is still a deep bargain, but its discount to the market has been narrowing. Caveat, clearly labeled: the BLS index tracks in-place metro rents and is smoother than Berkeley asking rents (which have actually softened since their mid-2010s peak), so treat it as a directional benchmark, not a Berkeley-specific quote.

IRS Form 990 History

The BSC files an annual IRS Form 990 as a nonprofit (EIN: 94-0948140). Source: ProPublica ↗

⚠ Do not use 990 figures to assess whether the BSC is financially healthy.

The 990 is a federal tax filing, not an operational report. It uses GAAP accounting, which differs significantly from the BSC's cash-basis operating budget. Capital expenditures like roof replacements are spread over decades as depreciation rather than recorded as a full expense in the year they occur, making annual expenses appear lower than they are in cash terms. Investment income and property appreciation are also included, so a strong endowment year can produce a 990 "surplus" even when the operating budget is running a deficit.

The net assets figure ($21–44M) reflects the BSC's buildings and real estate, not liquid reserves. The BSC can show a 990 surplus while simultaneously drawing down operating reserves to cover real cash shortfalls.

The operating budget is the correct document for assessing financial health. The board-approved FY 2026-27 budget projects a deficit of −$1,301,193, to be covered by drawing from operating reserves, regardless of what the 990 may show.

Key Terms & Glossary

Room

The housing fee paid by members living in one of the 17 BSC co-op houses. "Room" is effectively rent for a bed in a co-op house. It is charged per semester alongside the Board (food) fee, together they make up the full Room & Board (R&B) charge. Room revenue is the BSC's single largest revenue line.

Board

The food fee paid by members living in the 17 BSC co-op houses. Board covers communally prepared meals through the Cooperative Food System (CFS). Apartment residents (Rochdale, Fenwick, NSC) pay no board fee, they cook for themselves and are not part of the food system.

Rent (apartments)

The charge paid by members in the three BSC apartment buildings: Rochdale Village, Fenwick, and Northside (NSC). Apartment members pay rent only, there is no food fee. This is distinct from the "Room" fee paid by co-op house members.

Room & Board (R&B)

The combined housing + food charge for co-op house members. "Room" = the bed/housing fee. "Board" = the food fee. The per-semester R&B rate is set annually by the Board of Directors. FY 2026-27: $4,986/semester (up 7.5%). Apartment members do not pay a Board fee, they pay rent only.

Workshift

Required labor hours each member contributes weekly: cooking, cleaning, maintenance. Typically 5 hrs/week. This is what keeps BSC costs below market rates.

CFS

Cooperative Food System: the BSC's centralized food procurement and distribution network serving all houses.

Capital Improvement (CI)

Capital Improvement covers major building upgrades, structural repairs, and renovations that go beyond routine maintenance, things like roof replacements, electrical upgrades, seismic retrofits, and kitchen overhauls. CI projects are planned years in advance through the BSC's capital planning process and require Board approval. In the operating budget, CI appears as a direct expense line; on the IRS 990 (GAAP), these costs are capitalized as assets and depreciated over their useful life, which is why 990 figures look different. FY 2026-27 focus: roof replacements at Hillegass Parker (HiP) and Rochdale Village, two of the BSC's largest and oldest buildings. At $1,281,000, CI is one of the largest single expense lines in the budget.

Financing

Debt service: interest and principal payments on BSC loans and mortgages. Declining as debts are paid down.

Bad Debts

The amount written off when member charges cannot be collected. For co-op house members this includes unpaid Room (housing) and Board (food) fees; for apartment members it is unpaid Rent. When a member stops paying and recovery through payment plans, collections, or legal action fails, the balance is booked as a bad debt. Legal fees and bad debts tend to move together, as the same non-payment situations often generate costs in both lines. Up 121% to $210,000 in FY 2026-27.

VPCF

Vice President of Capital & Finance: the elected board member who authors the annual budget. FY 2026-27: Adrian Meneses (Euclid).

Evans Manor / Barrington

A property previously leased to the BSC, generating ~$434K/yr in lease income. The lease ended, eliminating this revenue stream.

Convent Rate

A monthly parking rate ($30/mo FY2016-17 → $49.69/mo projected FY2026-27) for the BSC-leased Convent property. Not a housing rate.

Hillegass Parker (HiP)

Hillegass Parker House: a BSC co-op limited to graduate and re-entry (age 25+) students. 57 residents, all singles (33 standard, 24 deluxe).

Form 990

Annual IRS filing required of nonprofits. 990 figures differ from operating budgets: they use GAAP accounting (capitalizing capital expenses, including investment income).

Accufund

The BSC's accounting software. The prior-year columns in each annual budget file pull from Accufund and represent real actuals.

NSC

Northside Cooperative: 26 residents in 1BR apartments and studios near the north side of campus. Graduate/re-entry focused.

Capital Improvement (CI)

Major building upgrades, structural repairs, and renovations beyond routine maintenance. Planned years in advance, requires Board approval.

Sources

All budget figures come from board-approved BSC budget documents. 990 data from ProPublica's Nonprofit Explorer.
