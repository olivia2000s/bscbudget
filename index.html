What is this, and how do I read it?

If you're new to BSC finances, read this section first. It explains what the BSC is, what a budget is, and what the key terms mean.

What is the BSC?

The Berkeley Student Cooperative is a student-run nonprofit that provides affordable housing and meals to UC Berkeley students. It has been operating since 1933 and is the largest student housing cooperative in the United States.
  • Members govern the BSC directly — each house elects officers, and an elected Board of Directors oversees the whole organization.
  • Members contribute labor (workshifts — cooking, cleaning, maintenance) in exchange for below-market housing costs.
  • The BSC is financially self-sustaining. Revenue comes from member room charges, meal plans, and apartment rent. There is no university subsidy.

What is a budget, and why does it matter to the Board?

A budget is a plan for money. It estimates how much the BSC expects to earn and spend over the fiscal year (Summer through Spring). It's approved before the year starts. This site shows actuals for all completed years (taken from the prior-year columns in each BSC budget file, which reflect real accounting data) and a projection for FY 2026-27 only.

The budget matters to the Board because it's the primary tool for financial oversight. When actual spending diverges from the budget, that's a signal to ask questions. The Board's job is to monitor the long-run trend and hold leadership accountable for closing the deficit over time.

What's the 2026-27 bottom line — and what does the history show?

The FY 2026-27 budget projects spending $1.30M more than revenue. This is a forward-looking estimate — it has not happened yet. Historically, the BSC ran small surpluses through most of 2007–2017. Deficits began appearing around 2013-14 and grew sharply after 2022. The Board should monitor whether the projected improvement from −$1.72M to −$1.30M actually materializes.

Revenue · FY 2026-27 Projections

Projected revenue

The BSC earns money from four main sources: room charges, meal plans, apartment rent, and parking. All figures below are FY 2026-27 projections.

How does BSC revenue work?

Members pay each semester for housing and meals. The BSC sets rates annually. This budget reflects a 7.5% increase on room, board, and rent, and 10% on parking. Revenue also depends on occupancy — how many beds are actually filled. This budget assumes: Summer 2026: 43% · Fall 2026: 90% · Spring 2027: 84.75%. If actual occupancy falls short, revenue will be lower than projected.

Glossary

Key terms explained

Financial terms that appear often in BSC budget discussions, in plain English.

Budget

A plan for money — what we expect to earn and spend. Approved before the year starts. Actual results always differ.

Projection / Estimate

A forward-looking number. FY 2026-27 figures are projections. All prior years are actual board-approved budgets.

Revenue

Money coming in — from member room and board payments, apartment rent, and parking fees.

Expenses

Money going out — staff wages, food, utilities, maintenance, insurance, debt service, and more.

Deficit / Loss

When expenses exceed revenue. The BSC projects a −$1.30M deficit for FY 2026-27. Would be covered by reserves.

Reserves

Savings for emergencies or future investments. The BSC has Operating, Scholarship, Growth, and Contingency funds. No contributions this year.

Occupancy

The percentage of BSC beds that are filled. Higher occupancy = more revenue. The budget assumes 90% in fall.

VPCF

Vice President of Central Finance — the elected student officer who prepares and manages the budget. Currently Adrian Meneses (Euclid house).

Convent Rate

A monthly parking rate ($30/mo in FY16-17, $49.69/mo projected FY26-27) for the BSC-leased Convent property. Not a housing rate. The Convent lease expired 12/31/2026.

Workshift

Labor members contribute to their co-op (cooking, cleaning, maintenance) in exchange for below-market housing.

Bad Debts

A reserve for members who don't pay. Paying members effectively cover the cost of non-payers.

Capital Improvement

Major infrastructure investments — roof repairs, HVAC, structural upgrades. Large one-time costs.

COLA

Cost of Living Adjustment — a pay increase tied to inflation. Staff received 2% COLA this year.

VPCF notes on key FY 2026-27 decisions

Payroll capped at $4.15M — Per Board directive; includes 2% COLA. Down $198K from prior year.
Evans Manor lease ended — The $434K annual lease income from Barrington is gone. Revenue comparisons vs. prior years are understated.
Garbage costs surged 63% — The BSC was already $46K over budget on garbage in FY 2025-26. The new $353K reflects reality.
Bad debts reserve doubled — $210K (up 121%) as a buffer for non-paying members. Paying members subsidize non-payers.
No reserve contributions — Operating Reserve, Scholarship Fund, Growth Fund, and Contingency Fund all receive $0 this year.
Convent lease & laundry — The BSC leases the Convent property. The lease expired 12/31/2026; the budget uses a rate of $1.01/sqft through that date. A new $45K laundry contract is also included in this line, making laundry free to members.
Financing costs declining — Down $197K from prior year. Debts are being paid down over time.

IRS Form 990 vs. operating budget

The BSC files a Form 990 with the IRS each year (fiscal year ending May 31). The 990 figures below come from ProPublica's Nonprofit Explorer and are the most authoritative public record of BSC finances. They differ significantly from operating budgets — see the explanation below.

⚠️
Why 990 and budget figures differ: The 990 (GAAP basis) includes investment income, donations, and asset sales in revenue, but capitalizes capital expenditures and principal repayments rather than expensing them. The operating budget includes capital projects and debt payments as expenses but excludes investment/donation income. Both are correct — they answer different questions. The budget reflects cash management pressure; the 990 reflects GAAP financial position.

Why 990 and operating budget figures differ

990 Revenue is HIGHER because it includes:

  • Investment income on BSC reserves ($200K–$600K/yr)
  • Contributions and donations from alumni
  • Proceeds from asset sales

990 Expenses are LOWER because it excludes:

  • Capital improvements (capitalized as assets under GAAP)
  • Debt principal repayments (balance sheet item)
  • These together total $2M+ per year in the operating budget

The 990 showing a surplus does not mean the BSC has cash to spare. The operating budget deficit reflects real financial pressure on day-to-day operations. The Board uses operating budget figures to set rates and manage spending — not the 990.
